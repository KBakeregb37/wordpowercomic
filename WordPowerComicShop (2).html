HOW TO USE THIS SITE

  • Your child picks a reading hero first, then chooses a comic book from the store.
  • Each comic has a short story followed by questions. Let your child try every question on their own first.
  • Yellow highlighted words can be clicked to hear the sounds — one at a time, never overlapping.
  • The syllable clapping demo shows each word part with one clapping hand. At the end of each row, a yellow box shows the total syllable count — for example: LI + ON = 2 SYLLABLES.
  • If your child gets stuck, encourage them to re-read the story before answering.
IMPORTANT — AUDIO NOTE FOR PARENTS:
Sound may lag by a few seconds on some browsers (especially Chrome on first use). This is completely normal. Please tell your child: "Click the yellow word once and then wait — the sound will come in just a moment!" Do not click again right away. The audio engine needs a brief moment to start up. If no sound plays after 8 seconds, try clicking the word one more time.

HELPING READERS WHO FORGET WHAT THEY READ

  • Read one panel at a time. Cover the other panels with your hand or a piece of paper. Looking at one chunk at a time prevents visual overwhelm.
  • Pause after every panel and ask: "What just happened?" This builds the habit of checking understanding before moving on.
  • Point to words as you read. Using a finger or a bookmark as a pointer helps anchor attention and prevents losing place on the page.
  • Re-read without shame. Normalize going back: "Let's read that one more time together." Re-reading is a skill, not a failure.
  • Tell the story back. After reading, ask your child to tell you what happened in their own words. If they get stuck, show them the picture panels as memory cues — not the words.
  • Connect to real life. "That reminds me of when you..." Personal connections make text stick in memory much longer.
  • Keep sessions short. 10–15 minutes of focused reading is more effective than a long session for children who struggle with sustained attention.
  • Celebrate effort, not just answers. "I love how hard you thought about that!" matters more than whether the answer was correct.
  • Use the speech bubbles. When a child forgets what they read, point back to the speech bubble and say: "Let's see what the character said right here." The visual structure of comics supports working memory.

ASK YOUR CHILD

  • "What happened to the hero in the story?"
  • "Can you find a word that rhymes with cat?"
  • "Clap the syllables in your own name for me!"
  • "Why did that happen in the story?"
  • "How do you think the character was feeling?"
  • "What is the most important idea in the story?"
  • "Can you spot a sight word on this page right now?"

AT-HOME STRATEGIES

  • Read each panel slowly together — pause and describe what you see in the picture.
  • Cover the words and ask: "What do you think is happening here?"
  • Clap syllables in everyday words at meals, on car rides, or during daily routines.
  • Point out sight words on cereal boxes, road signs, and store labels.
  • Ask "why did that happen?" about everyday events — cause and effect in real life!
  • Face emojis in the panels show feelings. Ask your child: "What is this character feeling, and how do you know?"
  • Speech bubbles show what characters SAY. Thought bubbles show what they THINK. Discuss the difference!

ABOUT THIS PROGRAM

All activities align to Georgia ELA standards (ELAGSEKRF2.a-e, RF4, ELAGSE2RI1-6). The site uses multi-sensory, low-distraction design: one sound at a time, consistent panel layouts, visual progress, movement-based syllable clapping, and click-to-hear audio. Hero choice and story path choice support autonomy and motivation for all learners.
